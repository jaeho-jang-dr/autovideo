1. The Birth and Uniqueness of Hangeul

Hangeul holds a unique position in the history of the world's writing systems. Unlike most traditional alphabets that evolved slowly over centuries, Hangeul is the only script where both the creator and the year of creation (1443) are explicitly known.

Featural Alphabet

Modern linguists classify Hangeul as a 'Featural Alphabet'. This means the shapes of the letters themselves visually represent the movements of the vocal organs and philosophical elements, rather than just representing arbitrary sounds.

King Sejong the Great created this script for commoners who were suffering from unfairness because they could not read complex Chinese characters. Initially named 'Hunminjeongeum' ("The Proper Sounds to Instruct the People"), it was designed so that a wise person could learn it in a single morning, and even a less clever person within ten days.

\n

1.1. Shape Representation of Consonants

The five basic Hangeul consonants (ㄱ, ㄴ, ㅁ, ㅅ, ㅇ) were modeled after the shapes of the speech organs (tongue, lips, teeth, and throat) during pronunciation. This scientific approach directly maps the physical point of articulation to the visual shape of the letter.

Basic Letter Vocal Organ Category Visual Modeling Origin
ㄱ Velar (아음) The tongue root blocking the throat (back of tongue touching the soft palate)
ㄴ Alveolar (설음) The tongue touching the upper gums (tip of tongue touching the roof behind upper teeth)
ㅁ Bilabial (순음) The shape of the lips (modeled as a square closed mouth)
ㅅ Dental (치음) The shape of a tooth (modeled as a sharp tooth outline)
ㅇ Glottal (후음) The shape of the throat (modeled as a round throat opening)

From these five basic shapes, other consonants are developed by adding strokes or doubling letters to represent stronger sounds, creating a highly structured phonetic grid.

\n

1.2. The Heaven-Earth-Human (Cheon-Ji-In) Principle

Hangeul vowels were modeled after the three main components of East Asian philosophy: Heaven (天), Earth (地), and Human (人). This reflects King Sejong's desire to incorporate universal harmony and human-centric design directly into the letters.

•
Heaven (Cheon)

Modeled after the round sky, represented as a single dot. (In modern Hangeul, this is written as a short horizontal or vertical tick)

ㅡ
Earth (Ji)

Modeled after the flat ground, represented as a horizontal line.

ㅣ
Human (In)

Modeled after a standing human being, represented as a vertical line.

By combining these three elements, secondary vowels (ㅏ, ㅓ, ㅗ, ㅜ) and tertiary double-vowels (ㅑ, ㅕ, ㅛ, ㅠ) are systematically derived, illustrating a beautiful geometric simplicity.

\n

1.3. The Stroke Addition Principle

Hangeul consonants expand by adding strokes to represent an increase in phonetic intensity. This 'Stroke Addition Principle' ensures that a reader can immediately tell the voice characteristic of a letter just by looking at its structure.

ㄱ (Plain) ➡️ ㅋ (Aspirated / 거센소리)
ㄴ (Plain) ➡️ ㄷ (Plain / 가획) ➡️ ㅌ (Aspirated / 거센소리)
ㅁ (Plain) ➡️ ㅂ (Plain / 가획) ➡️ ㅍ (Aspirated / 거센소리)
ㅅ (Plain) ➡️ ㅈ (Plain / 가획) ➡️ ㅊ (Aspirated / 거센소리)
ㅇ (Plain) ➡️ ㆆ (Outdated) ➡️ ㅎ (Aspirated / 거센소리)

This stroke-adding structure highlights that Hangeul is not a set of arbitrary symbols, but a phonetically engineered system with structural symmetry.

2. Pronunciation Guide for Non-Native Learners

When learning Hangeul, the primary obstacle for English speakers is the distinction between consonants and vowels. Standard Romanization fails to capture the physical reality of Korean pronunciation, leading to accented or incorrect speech.

Pedagogical Focus

Rather than just repeating after audio recordings, learners should focus on three components: Mouth Shape, Jaw Position, and Airflow Intensity. Providing visual and physical cues makes pronunciation intuitive.

Sori Hangeul combines 3D mouth-shape diagrams, physical feedback exercises, and instant audio feedback to build correct pronunciation habits from day one. The next pages explore consonants and vowels in detail.

\n

2.1. The 3-way Consonant Split

While English relies on a 2-way distinction between voiced and voiceless sounds (e.g., /g/ vs /k/), Korean 자음 has a unique 3-way distinction based on aspiration (airflow) and vocal cord tension.

Category Phonetic Quality Letters Tips for English Speakers
Plain (평음) Relaxed, soft release of air ㄱ, ㄷ, ㅂ, ㅅ, ㅈ Sounds halfway between English 'g/d/b' and 'k/t/p'. Understood as voiceless at the beginning of words but voiced between vowels.
Aspirated (격음) Strong, rapid burst of air ㅋ, ㅌ, ㅍ, ㅊ, ㅎ Hold a tissue or palm in front of the mouth. Learners should feel a "strong puff of air hitting their palm" upon articulation.
Tense (경음) Tightened vocal cords, no air release ㄲ, ㄸ, ㅃ, ㅆ, ㅉ Block all airflow and tighten the throat. Similar to the 'k' sound in 'sky' or 'p' sound in 'spy' (un-aspirated stops).

Practice: Contrast '방-팡-빵' (room-pop-bread) and '개-캐-깨' (dog-dig-sesame).

\n

2.2. Vowel Distinctions (으 / 어 / 오 / 우)

These four simple vowels are the most challenging for foreigners. Proper mouth shapes and jaw heights are critical.

으 [ɯ]

This sound does not exist in English. To pronounce it, keep your teeth close together and "make a forced smile shape" by stretching your lips wide to the sides, then release the sound from the throat.

어 [ʌ] vs 오 [o]

어: Drop your jaw wide vertically, keeping your lips flat and unrounded (unrounded vowel).
오: Round your lips tightly, forming a circle as if whistling or holding a straw (rounded vowel).

우 [u] vs 오 [o]

우: Purse your lips tightly and push them forward in a small, tight circle.
오: Relax the pursing slightly, dropping the jaw a bit more to make a larger circular opening.

3. Syllable Blocks and Writing Rules

Hangeul uses a "Moasseugi" (Syllable Block Assembly) layout where letters are grouped into square structures, rather than listed linearly like Western alphabets. This layout aids visual word recognition and speeds up reading.

Horizontal Vowels (ㅣ-style)
Initial (C)
Vowel (V)

Examples: 가, 나, 다, 라

Vertical Vowels (ㅡ-style)
Initial (C)
Vowel (V)

Examples: 고, 노, 두, 루

When teaching block assembly, visually mapping vowels as vertical walls or horizontal floors helps learners understand where to snap them relative to the initial consonant block.

3.1. Batchim (Final Consonants) & 7 Sounds

A consonant placed at the bottom of a syllable block is called **'Batchim' (받침)**. Although almost all consonants can sit in the Batchim slot, they are restricted to only seven representative sounds when pronounced.

Representative Sound Written Consonants Examples & Realized Sound
[ㄱ] ㄱ, ㅋ, ㄲ, ㄳ, ㄺ 책 [책], 부엌 [부억], 밖 [박], 닭 [닥]
[ㄴ] ㄴ, ㄵ, ㄶ 문 [문], 앉다 [안따], 많다 [만타]
[ㄷ] ㄷ, ㅅ, ㅈ, ㅊ, ㅌ, ㅎ, ㅆ 듣다 [듣따], 옷 [옫], 낮 [낟], 꽃 [꼳], 밑 [믿]
[ㄹ] ㄹ, ㄼ, ㄽ, ㄾ, ㅀ 물 [물], 여덟 [여덜], 넓다 [널따]
[ㅁ] ㅁ, ㄻ 엄마 [엄마], 삶 [삼]
[ㅂ] ㅂ, ㅍ, ㅄ, ㄿ 밥 [밥], 앞 [압], 값 [갑]
[ㅇ] ㅇ 공 [공], 강 [강]

Rule: If a Batchim is followed by a syllable starting with a vowel, the sound carries over (Liaison / 연음). E.g., 옷이 is pronounced as [오시].

3.2. Stroke Order Rules

Writing Hangeul requires following proper stroke order. Drawing letters arbitrarily leads to illegible handwriting, slow writing speeds, and hand fatigue. Proper stroke order optimizes hand flow and ensures aesthetic layout.

Two Basic Writing Principles

Rule 1. Left to Right: Draw all horizontal strokes and arrange components from left to right.
Rule 2. Top to Bottom: Draw all vertical strokes and stack components from top to bottom.

Example: How to write 'ㅁ' (Mium) in 3 strokes:

  1. Stroke 1: Draw the left vertical line downwards.
  2. Stroke 2: Start from top-left, draw rightwards, then hook downwards in a single motion.
  3. Stroke 3: Close the block with a horizontal line from left to right.

4. Short-Answer Practice Quiz

Test your Week 1 learning progress with these questions. Write your answers below.

Question 1. What are the three universal elements (Cheon-Ji-In) used to create Hangeul vowels, and what geometric shapes represent them?

Answer: Heaven (represented as a dot), Earth (horizontal line), and Human (vertical line).

Question 2. Explain the difference in mouth shape and jaw position between '어' [ʌ] and '오' [o].

Answer: '어' is flat-lipped (unrounded) with a low jaw drop. '오' requires rounding the lips forward with a less dropped jaw.

Question 3. What is the realized final sound (Batchim) for the word '값' (price)?

Answer: It is realized as [ㅂ], so the word is pronounced as [갑].

5. Essay and Discussion Topics

These topics encourage deeper reflection on the linguistic properties and design logic of Hangeul.

[Topic 1] Cognitive Ease of Featural Alphabets

Analyze how mapping letter shapes directly to vocal movements provides cognitive benefits for adult learners compared to non-featural writing systems (e.g., Latin alphabet). Cite specific examples like the stroke addition rule.

[Topic 2] Visualizing Vowel Harmony

East Asian Yin-Yang philosophy underpins Korean vowel harmony. Discuss how mapping these philosophical concepts to visual design elements (like warm red/orange blocks for Yang vowels, cool blue/navy blocks for Yin vowels) can make grammar rules intuitive for children.

6. Glossary and Learning Resources

  • Moasseugi (모아쓰기): The unique syllable block assembly format of Hangeul.
  • Featural Alphabet (자질문자): A writing system where letter shapes denote sound traits (like voicing, tension, or aspiration).
  • Batchim (받침): Final consonants placed at the bottom of a block.
  • Liaison (연음): The carrying over of a final consonant to the next vowel-initial syllable.
