Consonants

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=1

]

Korean has 19 consonant phonemes. 

[1]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Sohn-1

For each plosive and affricate, there is a three-way contrast between unvoiced segments, which are distinguished as plain, tense, and aspirated.

The "plain" segments, sometimes referred to as "lax" or "lenis", are considered to be the more "basic" or unmarked members of the Korean obstruent series. The "plain" segments are also distinguished from the 

tense

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tense_consonant

 and 

aspirated phonemes

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspirated_consonant

 by changes in 

vowel quality

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vowel_quality

, including a relatively lower 

pitch

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_frequency

 of the following vowel. 

[2]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Cho_Jun_Ladefoged-2

The "tense" segments, also referred to as "fortis", "hard", or "glottalized", have eluded precise description and have been the subject of considerable phonetic investigation. In the 

Korean alphabet

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_alphabet

 as well as all widely used 

romanization systems for Korean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Korean

, they are represented as doubled plain segments: ㅃ 

pp

, ㄸ 

tt

, ㅉ 

jj

, ㄲ 

kk

. As it was suggested from the 

Middle Korean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Korean

 spelling, the tense consonants came from the initial consonant clusters 

sC

-, 

pC

-, and 

psC

-. 

[3]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim-Renaud-3

 

[4]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Brown_Yeon-4

 

:29,38,452

The "aspirated" segments are characterized by 

aspiration

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspirated_consonant

, a burst of air accompanied by the delayed 

onset of voicing

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice-onset_time

.

Korean 

syllable structure

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllable_structure

 is maximally CGVC, where G is a glide /j, w, ɰ/. (There is a unique off-glide 

diphthong

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diphthong

 in the character 의 that combines the sounds [ɯ] and [i] creating [ɰ]). 

[5]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-:2-5

 Any consonant except /ŋ/ may occur initially, but only /p, t, k, m, n, ŋ, l/ may occur finally. Sequences of two consonants may occur between vowels.

Plain

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=2

]

/p, t, tɕ, k/ are voiced [b, d, dʑ, ɡ] between sonorants (including all vowels and 

certain consonants

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#Sonorants

) but voiceless elsewhere. Among younger generations, they may be just as aspirated as /pʰ, tʰ, tɕʰ, kʰ/ in initial position; the primary difference is that vowels following the plain consonants 

carry low tone

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonogenesis

. 

[6]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim_Beddor_Horrocks-6

 

[7]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Lee-K_Ramsey-7

Aspirated

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=3

]

/pʰ, tʰ, tɕʰ, kʰ/ are strongly aspirated, more so than English voiceless stops. They generally do not undergo intervocalic voicing, but a 2020 study reports that it still occurs in around 10 to 15% of cases. It is more prevalent among older male speakers who have aspirated stops voiced in as much as 28% of cases. 

[8]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-apparent-time-8

Tense

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=4

]

The 

IPA

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet

 diacritic ⟨ ◌͈⟩, resembling a subscript double straight quotation mark, shown here with a placeholder circle, is used to denote the tensed consonants /p͈/, /t͈/, /k͈/, /t͈ɕ/, /s͈/. Its official use in the 

Extensions to the IPA

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensions_to_the_IPA

 is for 

strong

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis

 articulation, but is used in literature in the context of Korean phonology for 

faucalized voice

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faucalized_voice

. 

[9]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-9

 

[

AI-retrievedsource

]

 The Korean consonants also have elements of 

stiff voice

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stiff_voice

, but it is not yet 

[

when?

]

 known how typical that is of faucalized consonants. Sometimes the tense consonants are marked with an apostrophe, ⟨ ʼ⟩, but that is not IPA usage; in the IPA, the apostrophe indicates 

ejective

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ejective

 consonants. Some works use full-size ʔ or small ˀ before tensed consonants; this notation is generally used to denote 

pre-glottalization

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-glottalization

. An asterisk * after a tensed consonant is also used in literature. 

[10]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Shin_Kiaer_Cha-10

They are produced with a partially constricted 

glottis

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glottis

 and additional subglottal pressure in addition to tense vocal tract walls, laryngeal lowering, or other expansion of the larynx.

An alternative analysis 

[11]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim_San-11

 proposes that the "tensed" series of sounds are (fundamentally) regular voiceless, unaspirated consonants: the "lax" sounds are voiced consonants that become devoiced initially, and the primary distinguishing feature between word-initial "lax" and "tensed" consonants is that initial 

lax

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lax_vowel

 sounds cause the following vowel to assume a low-to-high pitch contour, a feature reportedly associated with voiced consonants in many Asian languages (such as 

Shanghainese

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghainese

), whereas tensed (and also aspirated) consonants are associated with a uniformly high pitch.

Vowels before tense consonants (as well as aspirated) tend to be shorter than before lax stops. 

[10]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Shin_Kiaer_Cha-10

The 

Gyeongsang dialect

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyeongsang_dialect

 is known for realization of tense ㅆ 

ss

 as plain ㅅ 

s

.

Fricatives

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=5

]

ㅎ 

h

 does not occur in final position, 

[a]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-12

 though the sound /h/ does occur at the end of non-final syllables, where it affects the following consonant. (See below.) Intervocalically, it is realized as voiced [ɦ], and after voiced consonants it is either [ɦ] or silent. 

[10]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Shin_Kiaer_Cha-10

The analysis of /s/ as phonologically plain or aspirated has been a source of controversy in the literature.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Chang-13

 

[2]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Cho_Jun_Ladefoged-2

 Similarly to plain stops, it shows moderate aspiration word-initially but no aspiration word-medially. 

[2]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Cho_Jun_Ladefoged-2

 

[10]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Shin_Kiaer_Cha-10

 It also often undergoes intervocalic voicing. 

[2]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Cho_Jun_Ladefoged-2

 But similar to aspirated stops, it triggers high pitch in the following vowel.

Word-initial aspiration, intervocalic voicing, and higher pitch of the following vowels are shared qualities in Korean fricatives /s/ and /h/.

Sonorants

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=6

]

Sonorants resemble vowels in the sense that plain stops become voiced between a sonorant or a vowel and another vowel.

ㅁ, ㄴ /m, n/ tend to be 

denasalized

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denasalization

 word-initially. 

[13]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim-14

ㅇ 

ng

 does not occur in initial position, reflected in the way the Hangul 

jamo

 ㅇ has a different pronunciation in the initial position to the final position. These were distinguished when Hangul was created, with the 

jamo

 ㆁ with the upper dot and the 

jamo

 ㅇ without the upper dot; these were then conflated and merged in both the North Korean and South Korean standards. /ŋ/ can technically occur syllable-initially, as in 명이, which is written as /mjɔŋ.i/, but pronounced as /mjɔ.ŋi/.

ㄹ /l/ is an alveolar flap [ɾ] between vowels or between a vowel and an /h/. It is [l] or [ɭ] at the end of a word, before a consonant other than /h/, or next to another /l/; in these contexts, it is palatalized to [ʎ] before /i, j/ and before palatal consonant allophones. 

[14]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-15

 There is free variation at the beginning of a word, where this phoneme tends to become [n] before most vowels and silent before /i, j/, but it is commonly [ɾ] in English loanwords. Geminate /ll/ is realized as [ll]/[ɭɭ], or as [ʎʎ] before /i, j/. 

[15]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Lee-16

 

[16]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-17

In native Korean words, ㄹ 

r

 does not occur word initially, unlike in Chinese loans ( 

Sino-Korean vocabulary

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Korean_vocabulary

). 

[13]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim-14

 In South Korea, it is silent in initial position before /i/ and /j/, pronounced [n] before other vowels, and pronounced [ɾ] only in compound words after a vowel. The prohibition on word-initial 

r

 is called the 

initial sound rule

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_sound_rule

. Initial 

r

 is spelled with ㄹ in North Korea, but is often pronounced the same way as it is in South Korea.

"labour" ( 勞動) – North Korea: 

rodong

 ( 로동), South Korea: 

nodong

 ( 노동)

"history" ( 歷史) – North Korea: 

ryŏksa

 ( 력사), South Korea: 

yeoksa

 ( 역사)

This rule also extends to ㄴ 

n

 in many native and all Sino-Korean words, which is also lost before initial /i/ and /j/ in South Korean; again, North Korean preserves the [n] phoneme there.

"female" ( 女子) – North Korea: 

nyŏja

 ( 녀자), South Korea: 

yeoja

 ( 여자)

In both countries, initial 

r

When pronounced as an alveolar flap [ɾ], ㄹ is sometimes derived from [d] through a historical lenition process, such as in the verb 듣다 which turns into 들어요.

Consonant assimilation

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=10

]

As noted above, 

tenuis

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenuis_consonant

 stops and /h/ are voiced after the voiced consonants /m, n, ŋ, l/, and the resulting voiced [ɦ] tends to be elided. Tenuis stops become fortis after obstruents (which, as noted above, are reduced to [k̚, t̚, p̚]); that is, /kt/ is pronounced [k̚t͈]. On the other hand, fortis and nasal stops are unaffected by either environment, though /n/ assimilates to /l/ after an /l/. After /h/, tenuis stops become aspirated, /s/ becomes fortis, and /n/ is unaffected. 

[d]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-25

 Additionally, /l/ undergoes significant changes: it becomes [n] after all consonants except /n/ (which assimilates to /l/) or another /l/. For example, the word ⫽tɕoŋlo⫽ (종로) is pronounced /tɕoŋno/ (종노). 

[22]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-NHK-26

Korean also features regressive (anticipatory) assimilation, where a consonant tends to assimilate in 

manner

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manner_of_articulation

 but not in 

place of articulation

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Place_of_articulation

. For example, obstruents become nasal stops before nasal stops (which, as just noted, includes underlying ⫽l⫽), but do not change their position in the mouth. Velar stops (that is, all consonants pronounced [k̚] in final position) become [ŋ]; coronals ([t̚]) become [n], and labials ([p̚]) become [m]. For example, ⫽hankukmal

⫽ (한국말) is pronounced /hankuŋmal

/ (한궁말) (phonetically [hanɡuŋmal]). 

[22]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-NHK-26

Before the fricatives /s, s͈/, coronal obstruents assimilate to a fricative, resulting in a 

geminate

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geminate

. That is, ⫽tʰs⫽ is pronounced /ss͈/ ([s͈ː]). A final /h/ assimilates in both place and manner, so that ⫽h 

C

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consonant

 ⫽ is pronounced as a geminate (and, as noted above, aspirated if C is a stop). The two coronal sonorants, /n/ and /l/, in whichever order, assimilate to /l/, so that both ⫽nl⫽ and ⫽ln⫽ are pronounced [lː]. 

[22]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-NHK-26

There are lexical exceptions to these generalizations. For example, voiced consonants occasionally cause a following consonant to become fortis rather than voiced; this is especially common with ⫽ls⫽ and ⫽ltɕ⫽ as [ls͈] and [lt͈ɕ], but is also occasionally seen with other sequences, such as ⫽kjʌ.ulpaŋhak

⫽ ([kjʌulp͈aŋak̚

]), ⫽tɕʰamtoŋan

⫽ ([tɕʰamt͈oŋan

]) and ⫽wejaŋkanɯlo

⫽ ([wejaŋk͈anɯɾo

]). 

[22]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-NHK-26

Incorrect application of these phonological rules, such as improper nasalization or assimilation, can significantly impair intelligibility for native speakers. For instance, failing to apply nasalization rules correctly or not recognizing assimilation patterns can lead to pronunciations that are difficult for native speakers to understand.

Loss of vowel length contrast

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=13

]

Korean used to have a length distinction for each vowel, but this is now reported to be almost completely neutralized (though it is still prescriptive). 

[30]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim-Renaud_Tranter-35

 Long vowels were pronounced somewhat more peripherally than short ones. As an exception, for most of the speakers who still utilize vowel length contrastively, long /ʌː/ is actually [ɘː]. 

[15]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Lee-16

Vowel length is a remnant of rising tone, first emerging in Middle Korean. It was preserved only in initial syllables and was often neutralized, particularly in the following cases: 

[31]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-:0-36

In compound words:

사람 [sʰa̠ːɾa̠m] "man", but

눈사람 [nuːns͈a̠ɾa̠m] "snowman";

벌리다 [pɘːʎʎida̠] "to open, to spread", but

떠벌리다 [t͈ʌ̹bʌ̹ʎʎida̠] "to brag".

In most monosyllabic verbs when attaching a suffix starting with a vowel

굶다 [kuːmt͈a̠] "to starve", but

굶어 [kulmʌ̹];

넣다 [nɘːtʰa̠] "to put", but

넣으니 [nʌ̹ɯni]/[nʌ̹ɨni],

or a suffix changing transitivity

붇다 [puːt̚t͈a̠] "to swell up", but

불리다 [puʎʎida̠] "to soak";

꼬다 [k͈o̞ːda̠] "to twist", but

꼬이다 [k͈o̞ida̠] "to be entangled".

There were exceptions though:

얻다 [ɘːt̚t͈a̠] "to obtain" still had long vowels in

얻어 [ɘːdʌ̹];

없다 [ɘːp̚t͈a̠] "to not be" still had long vowels in

없으니 [ɘːp̚s͈ɯni]/[ɘːp̚s͈ɨni].

It has disappeared gradually among younger speakers, but some middle-aged speakers are still aware of it and can still produce it in conscious speech. The long–short merger has had two main aspects. The first is phonetic: The duration of long vowels in relation to short ones has reduced by a lot (from 2.5:1 in the 1960s to 1.5:1 in the 2000s). Some studies suggest that the length of all vowels is dependent on one's age (older speakers seem to exhibit a slower speech rate, and even their short vowels are produced relatively longer than those of younger speakers). The second aspect is lexical: The subset of words produced with long vowels has gotten smaller. Long vowels tend to be reduced most frequently in high-frequency words.

Traditionally, the Korean language has had strong

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vowel_harmony

Accent and pitch

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=15

]

In modern Standard Korean, in multisyllabic words the second syllable has high pitch that gradually comes down in subsequent syllables. The first syllable may have pitch as high as the second if it starts with a tense ㅃ, ㅉ, ㄸ, ㄲ, ㅆ /p͈, t͈ɕ, t͈, k͈, s͈/ or an aspirated ㅍ, ㅊ, ㅌ, ㅋ /pʰ, tɕʰ, tʰ, kʰ/ consonant, as well as ㅅ, ㅎ /sʰ, h/, or lower rising pitch if it starts with plain ㅂ, ㅈ, ㄷ, ㄱ /p, tɕ, t, k/ or a sonorant ㅁ, ㄴ, ㄹ /m, n, r/, including silent ㅇ, i.e. a vowel. 

[11]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim_San-11

As early as 2004, researchers have posited that pitch and voicing are the actual distinguishing features between modern Korean consonants rather than the standard but seemingly ill-defined "tense" vs "lax" theory. 

[11]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim_San-11

 A 2013 study by Kang Yoon-jung and Han Sung-woo which compared voice recordings of Seoul speech from speakers born between 1935 and 2005, and found that, especially among young women, 

lenis consonants

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis

 (ㅂㅈㄷㄱ), 

aspirated consonants

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspirated_consonant

 (ㅍㅊㅌㅋ) and fortis consonants (ㅃㅉㄸㄲ) had shifted from a distinction of 

voice onset time

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_onset_time

 to that of pitch, with aspirated consonants losing their aspiration and the higher pitch associated with aspirated consonants among older speakers becoming stronger, and suggests that the modern 

Seoul dialect

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seoul_dialect

 is currently undergoing 

tonogenesis

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonogenesis

. 

[32]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kang_Han_2013-39

 Kim Mi-Ryoung (2013) notes that these sound shifts still show variations among different speakers, suggesting that the transition is still ongoing. 

[33]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim2013_Tonogenesis-40

 Cho Sung-hye (2017) examined 141 Seoul dialect speakers, and concluded that these pitch changes were originally initiated by females born in the 1950s, and has almost reached completion in the speech of those born in the 1990s. 

[34]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Cho_2017-41

 However, Choi Ji-youn et al. (2020) found that the replacement of aspiration with high tone occurs only in the first syllable of a 

prosodic unit

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prosodic_unit

, with later syllables maintaining aspiration. 

[8]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-apparent-time-8

pitch accent

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_accent

 system. In the 

dialect of Northern Gyeongsang

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyeongsang_dialect

, in southeastern South Korea, any syllable may have pitch accent in the form of a high tone, as may the two initial syllables. In bisyllabic words, there are thus three tone patterns:

가지 

gáji

 [ká.dʑi] 'kind, sort'

가지 

gají

 [ka.dʑí] 'eggplant'

가지 

gájí

 [ká.dʑí] 'branch'

메누리

Age differences

[ 

edit

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Korean_phonology&action=edit&section=17

]

The following changes have been observed since the mid-20th century and by now are widespread, at least in South Korea.

Contrastive vowel length has disappeared. Although still prescriptive, in 2012, the vowel length was reported to have been almost completely neutralized in Korean, except for a very few older speakers of the Seoul dialect, 

[30]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim-Renaud_Tranter-35

 for whom the vowel length distinction was maintained only in the first syllable of a word. 

[36]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Lee-I_Ramsey-43

 Even amongst those middle-aged speakers who retain the distinction, the phonetic contrast between a long vowel and a short vowel has shrunk to 1.5:1, compared to 2.5:1 recorded in the 1960s; 

[31]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-:0-36

 additionally, the number of lexical items featuring long vowels has also reduced, with low-frequency words being more likely to retain long vowels than high-frequency ones. 

[31]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-:0-36

 Vowel length has subsequently become a 

prosodic feature

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)

 of the language, used mainly for emphasis, and placed typically on the first syllable of the word. 

[30]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim-Renaud_Tranter-35

The 

mid front rounded vowel

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mid_front_rounded_vowel

 ([ 

ø

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mid_front_rounded_vowel

] ㅚ) and the 

close front rounded vowel

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Close_front_rounded_vowel

 ([ 

y

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Close_front_rounded_vowel

] ㅟ), 

[29]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Ahn_Iverson-33

 

:6

 can still be heard in the speech of some older speakers, but they have been largely replaced by the diphthongs [we] and [ɥi], respectively. 

[4]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Brown_Yeon-4

 

:4–6

 In a 2003 survey of 350 speakers from Seoul, nearly 90% pronounced the vowel ㅟ as [ɥi]. 

[36]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Lee-I_Ramsey-43

The distinction between /e/ and /ɛ/ is lost in South Korean dialects. A number of homophones have appeared due to this change, and speakers may employ different strategies to distinguish them. For example, 내가 /nɛ-ɡa/ "I- 

subject

" and 네가 /ne-ɡa/ "you- 

subject

" are now pronounced as [ne̞ɡɐ] and [niɡɐ] respectively, with the latter having changed its vowel; 새 잔 /sɛ tɕan/ "new glass" is pronounced with tensified [s͈] by some young speakers to not be conflated with 세 잔 /se tɕan/ "three glasses".

Some changes are still ongoing. They depend on age and gender, the speech of young females tends to be most innovative, while old males are phonologically conservative.

Plain stops in word-initial position are becoming as aspirated as "true" aspirated stops. They are still distinguished by their pitch, 

[6]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim_Beddor_Horrocks-6

 which indicates ongoing 

tonogenesis

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonogenesis

 in Contemporary Seoul Korean. 

[32]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kang_Han_2013-39

 

[33]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Kim2013_Tonogenesis-40

 

[34]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-Cho_2017-41

 This is however contested by studies which explain this as a 

prosodic feature

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)

. 

[8]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-apparent-time-8

Some words experience tensification of initial plain consonants, in both native and Sino-Korean words. It is proscribed in normative Standard Korean, but may be widespread or occur in free variation in certain words. 

[37]

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_phonology#cite_note-44

 Examples:

가시 /kasi/ "1) thorn; 2) worm" is pronounced 까시 /k͈asi/

닦다 /tak̚t͈a/ "to polish" is pronounced 딲다 /t͈ak̚t͈a/

조금 /tɕoɡɯm/ "a little" is pronounced 쪼금 /t͈ɕoɡɯm/, 쬐끔 /t͈ɕʷek͈ɯm/

Tensification is very common in Western loanwords: 배지 [p͈e̞t͈ɕi] "badge", 버스 [p͈ʌ̹s͈ɯ] "bus", 잼 [t͈ɕe̞m] "jam", although also proscribed in South Korea.
