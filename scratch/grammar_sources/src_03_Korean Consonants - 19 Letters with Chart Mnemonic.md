Position Rules (Initial vs. Final Consonants)

In Korean, the sound of a consonant is dictated by its placement within a syllable block. A single letter can shift from a light, airy sound at the beginning of a block to a sharp, "clipped" stop at the bottom.

Initial Consonants

Every Korean syllable must begin with a consonant. When placed in this initial position, most consonants are relatively voiceless and short in duration. The letter 

ㅇ

 acts as a silent placeholder when a syllable starts with a vowel, while the letter 

ㅂ

 stands out as the primary exception that maintains a more "voiced" quality.

The Batchim Rule (Final Consonants)

The 

받침 (Batchim)

 refers to the consonant residing at the bottom of a syllable block. This position is crucial because it often simplifies the pronunciation; for instance, several different letters like 

ㅅ, ㅈ, ㅊ,

 and ** ㅌ** all compress into a simple "t" sound when placed here. These final sounds are "unreleased," meaning the airflow is cut off abruptly rather than exhaled.

While you may encounter complex "Double Batchim" combinations (such as 

ㄳ

 or 

ㄶ

), these follow specific priority rules where usually only one of the two letters is actually voiced. Understanding how final consonants interact with the rest of a word is the key to natural-sounding Korean. We cover this topic in detail in our dedicated guide on 

Batchim
