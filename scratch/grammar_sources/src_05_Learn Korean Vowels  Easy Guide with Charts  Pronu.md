Korean Vowels Explained (Quick Overview)

There are 

21 Korean vowels

 in total, which serve as the foundation of the Hangeul alphabet. These vowels are categorized into two main groups: 

10 basic vowels

 and 

11 combined vowels (diphthongs)

.

How to Say "Vowels" in Korean

You can say 

"vowels" in Korean

 as 모음 (moeum). These characters are part of the Korean alphabet that is used both in South Korea and North Korea.

 

The 21 Korean Vowels at a Glance

There are 21 vowels in the Korean language. Korean vowels can be categorized into 

basic vowels

 and 

double vowels

.

In the Korean writing system, vowels are positioned either to the 

right

 of or 

below

 the initial consonant. This depends on whether the vowel's shape is vertical (like ㅏ) or horizontal (like ㅗ).

How to Construct Syllables with Korean Vowels

Most of the Korean syllable construction with vowels is rather straightforward. To form syllables, you simply add the vowel after the consonant, including the soundless one, ㅇ. Remember that ㅇ is used as the first letter in a syllable in cases where the syllable begins with a vowel.

The Secret to Hangeul Shapes: Vertical vs. Horizontal

Before you start building words, you need to know that Korean vowels fall into two categories based on their shape. This shape tells you exactly where to place the vowel in relation to a consonant.

1. Vertical Vowels (The "Standing" Vowels)

These vowels are tall and skinny. They always stand to the 

right

 of the initial consonant.

Characters:

 ㅏ, ㅑ, ㅓ, ㅕ, ㅣ, ㅐ, ㅒ, ㅔ, ㅖ

2. Horizontal Vowels (The "Sitting" Vowels)

These vowels are wide and flat. They always sit 

directly below

 the initial consonant.

Characters:

 ㅗ, ㅛ, ㅜ, ㅠ, ㅡ

3. Mixed Vowels (The "L-Shaped" Vowels)

Double vowels that combine a horizontal and a vertical vowel (like ㅘ or ㅝ) wrap around the consonant, sitting both below and to the right.

Structure:

 과, 워, 왜

Additionally, if the Korean syllable has a final consonant, then another consonant will be added after the vowel. Otherwise, you move to build the next syllable.

Why the Placeholder (ㅇ) Matters

In Korean writing, a vowel must always be attached to a consonant to form a block. If a word or syllable starts with a vowel sound (like "ah"), you must add the silent consonant 

ㅇ

 to the block (forming 

아

). This maintains the structural balance of the Hangeul system.

 

Syllables With Double Vowels

In the case of double vowel letters starting with ㅗ, ㅜ, or ㅡ, the Korean consonant letter will be added above this portion of the vowel, while the latter part of the vowel combination is "leftover" as its own part of the syllable. For example, the verb 와 (wa) means "come" in the present casual tense.

It is also entirely possible for a Korean syllable to have one vowel (that is not a double vowel) with three consonant letters! But you will want to check the lesson for Korean consonant letters, starting with the basic letters, to learn more about this.

While the rules for building syllable blocks are logical, putting them into practice can be tricky for beginners. Many learners find that even when they know the rules, they still face hurdles when trying to sound natural.

To help you master the nuances of Hangeul, we've identified the top areas where students typically get stuck.

3 Common Mistakes Learners Make with Korean Vowels

Even if you memorize the charts, many learners struggle with the nuances of natural speech. These small details make a big difference:

Over-relying on Romanization

Writing Korean sounds with English letters is a helpful "training wheel," but it can be misleading. For example, ㅓ is often written as "eo," leading students to pronounce it like "ee-oh." In reality, it sounds much closer to the "u" in "up."

Confusing the "O" sounds (ㅓ vs ㅗ)

This is the most common hurdle. ㅓ (eo) is an "open" sound, and your mouth stays relaxed. ㅗ (o) is a "closed" sound, so you must purse your lips into a tight circle (like you're holding a straw).

Stress over ㅐ and ㅔ

Many learners spend hours trying to hear the difference. In modern Standard Korean, these two are pronounced almost identically.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Here are the commonly asked questions related to Korean vowels.

1. Can Korean vowels sound exactly the same even if they're spelled differently?

Yes! In modern Standard Korean, vowel pairs like 

ㅐ vs ㅔ

 and 

ㅙ vs ㅚ vs ㅞ

 are typically pronounced identically, despite different spellings.

2. Is there a difference between long vs short vowels in Korean?

No. Korean vowels are 

monophthongs

, meaning they don't vary in length like English “long a” or “short e.” Each vowel sound is stable and consistent

3. Are combined vowels just the two simple vowels said one after the other?

Yes, exactly. Pronouncing combined vowels like “ㅘ” (ㅗ + ㅏ) or “ㅢ” (ㅡ + ㅣ) effectively means starting with the first vowel, then transitioning into the second.

4. Do Korean syllables ever start with a vowel?

When a syllable begins with a vowel sound, it's always written with the 

silent consonant ㅇ

 first (e.g., 왜 is written as ㅇ + ㅙ).

5. Do vowel sounds change depending on their position or the surrounding letters?

Not typically! Korean vowels maintain roughly the same sound regardless of their position. They don't shift like English vowels do in words such as “take” or “cake."
