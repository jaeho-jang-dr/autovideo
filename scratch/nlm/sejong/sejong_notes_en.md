# Study Guide: The Creation, Structure, and Evolution of Hunminjeongeum

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of *Hunminjeongeum*, covering the historical context of its creation, the philosophical and linguistic principles behind the script's design, the structure of its primary explanatory text, and its subsequent evolution into modern Hangul.

---

## I. Historical Context and Timeline

The term *Hunminjeongeum* refers to two distinct entities: the writing system itself (the alphabet) and the book published to explain it. 

### Key Dates and Milestones

| Date | Event | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1443 (Sejong 25)** | Creation | King Sejong the Great personally created the 28-letter script. |
| **1444 (Sejong 26)** | Official Opposition | Choe Man-li, a scholar of the Hall of Worthies (*Jiphyeonjeon*), submitted a memorial opposing the use of the script. |
| **1446 (Sejong 28)** | Promulgation | The *Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon* (Explanatory Edition) was published in the 9th lunar month. |
| **1926** | First "Gagyanal" | The Korean Language Research Society established the first commemorative day for the script. |
| **1933** | Standardization | The Korean Language Society established the "Unified System of Hangeul Spelling," reducing the 28 letters to 24. |
| **1940** | Discovery of *Haeryebon* | A copy of the *Haeryebon* was discovered in Andong, Gyeongsangbuk-do, providing the definitive historical evidence for Hangul's design. |

## II. Structure of the Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon

The *Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon* is a woodblock-printed book consisting of 33 pages. It is divided into two primary sections: the *Yei* (Precepts) and the *Haerye* (Explanations).

### 1. Yei (Precepts)
Written by King Sejong himself, this section includes:
*   **The Preface:** Outlines the purpose and motivation for creating the script (54 characters).
*   **Sound Values and Usage:** Briefly explains the phonological values of the initial, medial, and final sounds and the rules for combining them.

### 2. Haerye (Explanations)
Written by eight scholars from the Hall of Worthies (including Jeong In-ji, Shin Suk-ju, and Seong Sam-mun), this section provides a detailed philosophical and linguistic analysis:
*   **Jeojahae (Explanations of Character Design):** Explains the principles of "Sangehyeong" (imitating shapes) and "Gaehui" (adding strokes).
*   **Choseonghae (Initial Consonants):** Detailed explanation of the 17 initial consonants.
*   **Jungseonghae (Medial Vowels):** Detailed explanation of the 11 medial vowels.
*   **Jongseonghae (Final Consonants):** Detailed explanation of the final consonants and the principle of "Jongseong buyong choseong" (finals reuse initials).
*   **Hapjabae (Combining Characters):** Instructions on how to combine initial, medial, and final sounds into a single syllabic block.
*   **Yongjarye (Examples of Usage):** Provides 123 examples of words using the script.
*   **Jeong In-ji's Postface:** A closing essay justifying the script's necessity based on the differences between Korean and Chinese phonology.

## III. Design Principles of the Script

### 1. Initial Consonants (Choseong)
The 17 initial consonants were designed based on the shape of the speech organs at the moment of articulation.
*   **Molar sounds (ㄱ):** Root of the tongue blocking the throat.
*   **Lingual sounds (ㄴ):** Tongue touching the upper gums.
*   **Labial sounds (ㅁ):** Shape of the mouth.
*   **Dental sounds (ㅅ):** Shape of the teeth.
*   **Guttural sounds (ㅇ):** Shape of the throat.
*   **Gaehui (Adding Strokes):** To indicate stronger sounds, strokes were added to basic forms (e.g., ㄴ → ㄷ → ㅌ).

### 2. Medial Vowels (Jungseong)
The 11 medial vowels were designed based on the three fundamental elements of the universe (*Cheon-ji-in*):
*   **Heaven (ㆍ):** A round dot representing the roundness of the sky.
*   **Earth (ㅡ):** A flat line representing the flat ground.
*   **Man (ㅣ):** A vertical line representing a standing human.

### 3. Final Consonants (Jongseong)
The script operates on the principle that the final sound does not require new letters. Instead, it reuses the initial consonant letters. While all initials could theoretically be used, the *Haeryebon* notes that eight characters (ㄱ, ㆁ, ㄷ, ㄴ, ㅂ, ㅁ, ㅅ, ㄹ) are sufficient for practical use in most cases (*Paljongseongbeop*).

## IV. Short-Answer Practice Questions

1.  **Who is credited with the physical calligraphy of the *Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon*?**
    *   *Answer:* Prince Anpyeong (Yong), the third son of King Sejong.
2.  **In which 16th-century text were the modern names of the consonants (e.g., Gi-yeok, Ni-eun) first recorded?**
    *   *Answer:* *Hunmong Jahoe* by Choe Se-jin.
3.  **What was the primary reason Choe Man-li opposed the use of the new script?**
    *   *Answer:* He argued it violated "Sino-centrism" (*Junghwa*), suggesting that creating a unique script was a move toward "barbarism" and would hinder the study of Chinese Confucian scholarship.
4.  **Name the four letters that were included in the original 28 characters but are no longer used in modern Hangul.**
    *   *Answer:* *Arae-a* (ㆍ), *Ban-chieum* (ㅿ), *Yet-ieung* (ㆁ), and *Yeorin-hieut* (ㆆ).
5.  **What is the meaning of the principle "Jongseong buyong choseong"?**
    *   *Answer:* It means "the final sounds reuse the initial sounds," indicating that no separate letters were created specifically for syllable-final consonants.

## V. Essay Prompts for Deeper Exploration

1.  **Phonological Logic vs. Philosophical Symbolism:** Analyze how *Hunminjeongeum* balances scientific observation (speech organ shapes) with Neo-Confucian philosophy (Heaven, Earth, Man). To what extent do these two frameworks complement or contradict each other in the script's design?
2.  **The Mystery of the Creation Process:** Based on the source context, evaluate the theory that King Sejong created the script in secret. Contrast the historical record of his personal creation (*Sangchinje*) with the role eventually played by the scholars of the Hall of Worthies in drafting the *Haeryebon*.
3.  **Evolution of Terminology:** Discuss the transition of the consonant names from the 16th-century *Hunmong Jahoe* to modern usage. How did the phonetic requirements of using Hanja to name Korean sounds result in the unique naming conventions we see today (such as *Gi-yeok* and *Di-geut*)?
4.  **The Impact of the 1940 Discovery:** Explore how the discovery of the *Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon* changed the historical understanding of Hangul. How does this primary source refute earlier myths regarding the script's origin?

## VI. Glossary of Important Terms

*   **Arae-a (ㆍ):** An original vowel representing "Heaven." It was the first of the four lost letters to be officially abolished (1912/1933).
*   **Bujehak:** The highest-ranking full-time official position at the Hall of Worthies (*Jiphyeonjeon*), held by Choe Man-li at the time of his opposition.
*   **Gaehui (加劃):** The principle of "adding strokes" to a basic character to represent a sound of greater intensity or strength.
*   **Haeryebon:** The "Explanatory Edition" of *Hunminjeongeum*, which contains the detailed linguistic and philosophical justifications for the script.
*   **Hapjabae:** The section of the *Haeryebon* explaining the "combination of characters" to form syllabic blocks.
*   **Hunmong Jahoe:** A 16th-century textbook by Choe Se-jin used to teach Hanja, notable for providing the first recorded names for the Korean consonants.
*   **Paljongseongbeop:** The "Rule of Eight Final Consonants," a simplified orthographic principle suggesting that only eight specific consonants are needed to represent all syllable-final sounds.
*   **Sangehyeong (象形):** The "imitation of shapes," the foundational design principle where letters were modeled after the speech organs or universal elements.
*   **Yeorin-hieut (ㆆ):** One of the four lost letters; it disappeared from literature as early as the 15th century as it had no distinct sound value in native Korean words.
*   **Yongjarye:** The "Usage Examples" section of the *Haeryebon*, providing 123 vocabulary words to demonstrate the script in practice.
