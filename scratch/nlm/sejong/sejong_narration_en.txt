[1] King Sejong, the fourth ruler of Joseon, firmly believed that the people are the foundation of the nation and that communication with them is paramount. However, the majority of the populace remained illiterate, unable to learn the complex Chinese characters. Countless commoners, unable to write, could not express their thoughts and had no way to appeal their grievances to the government.

[2] In 1428, when a tragic patricide occurred in Jinju, King Sejong lamented deeply. To educate the public, the king compiled and distributed "Samganghaengsildo," which illustrated the deeds of loyal subjects, filial sons, and virtuous women. However, the illiterate commoners could not fully comprehend the deep moral teachings of the book merely by looking at the pictures.

[3] Anticipating fierce opposition from his ministers, King Sejong secretly pushed forward the creation of the twenty-eight letters in his private chambers. In February 1444, upon learning of this, Associate Dean of Jiphyeonjeon Choi Man-ri and other officials submitted a strong opposing memorial, claiming it violated proprietary diplomacy with China and harmed Neo-Confucian studies. The king sharply rebuked their ignorance and, showing resolute determination, completed the creation after temporarily detaining them.

[4] The consonants, representing the initial sounds of Hangeul, were created by closely observing and mimicking the shapes of human vocal organs. The basic letters, such as the velar 'ㄱ' and alveolar 'ㄴ', symbolized the tongue root blocking the throat and the tongue touching the upper gum, respectively. By adding strokes to these basic letters as the sounds grew stronger, a highly scientific and systematic consonant structure was established.

[5] The vowels, representing the medial sounds, were modeled after Heaven, Earth, and Humanity—the three central elements of Eastern cosmic philosophy. They are embodied by the dot 'ㆍ' for the round sky, the flat line 'ㅡ' for the earth, and the vertical line 'ㅣ' for the standing human. By combining and synthesizing these three fundamental characters in accordance with the harmony of Yin and Yang, vowels that could express all sounds were born.

[6] In the winter of 1443, the original and magnificent twenty-eight new characters were quietly completed under the Joseon night sky. Three years later, in 1446, "Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon"—a historical commentary featuring the dedicated explanations and analyses of young Jiphyeonjeon scholars, including Jeong In-ji—was officially promulgated to the world.

[7] To prove the excellence and practicality of the new script, the Joseon court composed "Yongbieocheonga," the first Hangeul literature praising the founding of the dynasty. They also translated and published numerous Buddhist scriptures, including "Neung-eomgyeong Eonhae," to allow the public to learn truths and take them to heart. Furthermore, Hangeul was introduced into exams for low-ranking administrative clerks to accelerate its practical dissemination.

[8] Hangeul is a miraculous system of writing, being the only alphabet in the world whose creator, design principles, and date of promulgation are fully documented and preserved. Recognizing this unique value, "Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon" was officially inscribed on the UNESCO Memory of the World Register in 1997. Honoring King Sejong's deep love for his people, UNESCO annually awards the "King Sejong Literacy Prize" to those dedicated to eradicating illiteracy worldwide.

[9] In 1926, during the Japanese colonial rule, the Joseon Language Research Society declared the first Hangeul celebration, "Gagya Day," to prevent the extinction of the script and preserve the national spirit. Later, in 1940, with the dramatic discovery of the lost original Haeryebon in Andong, Gyeongsangbuk-do, the precise record of "early September" in Jeong In-ji's preface was recovered. Based on this historical evidence, Hangeul Day was officially established on October 9th by converting the lunar date to the solar calendar.

[10] Today, Hangeul stands as Korea's unique cultural heritage and a great, shared asset used by both North and South Korea. Although four of the original twenty-eight characters, including the Arae-a, have disappeared over time, the remaining fourteen consonants and ten vowels shine brilliantly as the most rapid and accurate writing system optimized for the digital information age. King Sejong's noble spirit of loving his people—hoping for a world free of grievances—continues to breathe in every single stroke we write today.
