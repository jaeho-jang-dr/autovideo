[1] Since my childhood, I have loved reading so much that I never let a book leave my hand day or night. When I open a book and engrave the words of the sages onto my heart, all the worries of the world disappear and my heart flutters. Even when my older brothers played in the courtyard, I quietly sat in my room, making friends with books and exploring learning.

[2] My father appointed excellent teachers for my older brothers, Yangnyeong and Hyoryeong, and me to open the path of learning. While my two brothers enjoyed archery and horseback riding, I found the greatest joy in learning and discussing the classics with our teachers. I always knelt and listened closely, determined not to miss even a single character or stroke of their teachings.

[3] Because of my obsessive immersion in books, I eventually developed a severe eye disease and fell ill with a fever from overwork. My father, deeply concerned for me, issued a strict order to a young eunuch to take away and hide all the books in my room. Losing all my precious books in an instant, my heart felt incredibly sad and empty.

[4] Although my father hid all the books, I accidentally discovered a single copy of "Guso-sugan" left in a narrow corner behind the folding screen. Overjoyed as if I had won the entire world, I read and reread this precious book, forgetting my illness. Every single moment of turning the pages alone under the dim light was supreme happiness for me.

[5] My father always taught me that cultivating and straightening the mind is the most important task before decorating one's appearance. Following this teaching, I would maturely advise even the Crown Prince, my eldest brother, to align his mind first before grooming his looks. The reason I love learning is to bring peace to my family and nation, and to benefit the people.

[6] Even after growing into a Grand Prince, my thirst for learning grew even deeper. Borrowing all the classics and ancient texts in the palace to read day and night was my sole pleasure. Instead of political ambition, my quiet study in pursuit of truth was the most peaceful sanctuary for my soul.

[7] As the third son of my father, King Taejong, I was set apart from the heavy burden of royal succession. While my older brothers pursued their own paths around the periphery of power, I was able to lead a calm and peaceful life as a scholar prince. This solitary yet free time was an immense blessing to me.

[8] At the age of twelve, I wed the warm and virtuous daughter of Lord Shim On, who was two years my senior. Although our union was arranged by our families, our gentle natures harmonized perfectly, allowing us to build a calm marital affection. At times, to purify our minds, we would visit quiet temples together and pray, enduring the turbulent winds of the era.

[9] Before long, our modest residence was filled with the bright laughter of our young children. Watching them grow—from our eldest daughter, Princess Jeongso, to our firstborn son, Hyang, and our brilliant second daughter, Princess Jeong-ui—was a wonder of the world unlike any book. Holding these children in my arms, I realized the true value of life as a benevolent father and a human being.

[10] I believed that cultivating humility and treating others with benevolence, rather than climbing to the pinnacle of power, was the true teaching of the sages. Instead of being arrogant by flaunting my status as a grand prince or my family's influence, I always strove to polish my mind in modesty and respect everyone around me. Although I was far from the throne, within this reconstruction of a peaceful daily life, I only prayed deeply for the tranquility of the nation and the comfort of the people.

[11] In the autumn of 1418, I accepted the abdication of my father, King Taejong, and ascended the throne as the fourth king of Joseon at the young age of twenty-two. The weight of the crown was immensely heavy, and the profound anxiety and responsibility of how to establish the foundation of the nation under the shadow of the retired king oppressed my shoulders.

[12] In the second year of my reign, I established Jiphyeonjeon, an academic research institute, within the palace and gathered young scholars of outstanding ability and conduct. This marked the beginning of my long-cherished desire to implement a wise governance by constantly discussing and communicating with my ministers to establish proper systems.

[13] The people are the foundation of the nation, and it was my firm belief that only when the foundation is strong can the nation be at peace. To stabilize the state and make the lives of my subjects practically convenient and prosperous, I devoted myself day and night to governing and caring for the public welfare.

[14] However, the lives of the subjects I witnessed in reality were truly miserable, trapped in the darkness of illiteracy. Faced with difficult Chinese legal documents at government offices, commoners could not write a single character to appeal their grievances, often leading them to make false confessions or weep helplessly.

[15] When a tragic patricide occurred, I fell into deep grief, believing it was because the people had not learned the basic morals of Confucianism. Consequently, I instructed the scholars of Jiphyeonjeon to compile "Samganghaengsildo," which illustrated the exemplary deeds of loyal subjects, filial sons, and virtuous women with both text and drawings, and had it widely printed and distributed.

[16] However, "Samganghaengsildo," created with all our strength to educate the people, remained a high barrier for the illiterate subjects. Unable to read Chinese characters, the pitiful commoners could not comprehend the deep moral principles alongside the illustrations, and I keenly realized that pictures alone could not fully rescue them. It was the moment I recognized the desperate need for an easy script they could read and write themselves.

[17] Whenever I tended to state affairs, the sorrowful tears of the illiterate subjects weighed heavily on my heart. Since all court trial documents were written in complex Chinese characters, the people often suffered unjust punishments or made false confessions without even knowing the weight of their alleged crimes. To rescue them, I spent countless days and nights contemplating how to first translate and distribute these laws using Idu.

[18] When a man named Kim Hwa murdered his father in Jinju in 1428, my heart felt as if it were torn to pieces. To educate the ignorant subjects who knew no morals, I compiled and distributed "Samganghaengsildo," but the commoners remained illiterate. Witnessing that pictures alone could not enlighten them with deep moral truths, I firmly resolved to grant them our own easy characters that they could read alongside the illustrations.

[19] Fully anticipating the fierce opposition of the literati, this task was entirely a solitary, secret project carried out alone within my private chambers. Even as severe diabetes and eye diseases clouded my vision and boils tormented my entire body, I could not lay down my brush. Guided by a profound knowledge of phonology that far surpassed that of the scholars, I unlocked the long-closed latches of Eastern sounds all by myself.

[20] In the winter of the Gyehae year, 1443, I finally completed and proclaimed to the world the great twenty-eight characters, corresponding to the twenty-eight heavenly constellations. These letters, mimicking the ancient seal script, were divided into initial, medial, and final sounds, capable of transcribing both our national dialect and all natural sounds. I named this system "Hunminjeongeum," meaning the correct sounds to instruct the people.

[21] In February 1444, immediately after creating the letters, I instructed the young Jiphyeonjeon scholars to gather at the Uisacheong and translate "Go-Geum-Un-Hoe-Geo-Yo," the essential rhyme-book of the era, using our new script. I appointed Crown Prince Hyang, Grand Prince Jinyang, and Grand Prince Anpyeong to directly supervise this critical enterprise. This was my meticulous first step toward practical implementation, testing whether the new letters could standardize Chinese characters.

[22] As soon as the news of translating the rhyme-book spread, Jiphyeonjeon Associate Dean Choi Man-ri, whom I had long trusted, along with other ministers, submitted a fierce memorial of opposition. Fearing that the ruling class order would crumble, they criticized me directly, claiming that creating a separate phonetic script violated propriety and our duty to serve China. In their outdated eyes, not a single shred of compassion for the pitiful subjects could be found.

[23] Choi Man-ri argued that while Joseon had always followed the Chinese systems, creating a separate script was a path toward turning ourselves into barbarians like the Mongols, Jurchens, or Japanese. He also condemned me, asserting that Seol Chol's Idu was already sufficient and widely used in offices and among the public, so there was no need to harm Neo-Confucian studies by creating such a vulgar and easy new script. Confronting their elitist stubbornness to protect their privileges, I could hardly contain my boiling rage.

[24] The ministers insisted that the occurrence of unjust trials was solely due to unfair officials, not because the people were illiterate. They lamented that if the characters were too easy, the lazy populace would abandon the classics of the sages and only learn the vulgar script, becoming blind to right and wrong. Their words were filled with nothing but the cowardly resistance of the privileged class wishing to monopolize the power of literacy.

[25] At last, unable to hold back, I sternly rebuked them. "Do you even know the rhyme-books? Do you have any idea how many consonants and vowels exist in the four tones and seven sounds? You deem it right for Seol Chol to have created Idu to benefit the people, so why do you declare it wrong for your sovereign to create letters to comfort his subjects!" If I do not rectify this phonology, who under the heavens of Joseon will ever straighten the voices of the people!

[26] In the end, showing no compromise, I immediately imprisoned Choi Man-ri and the seven opposing officials in the Uigeumbu. I severely punished Kim Mun with flogging for changing his stance, and ruthlessly dismissed Jeong Chang-son for insulting the populace. Though I released them the following day, I could never yield to any minister's stubbornness when it came to this great task of benefiting the people.

[27] The consonants were created by mimicking the physical shapes of human vocal organs, containing the very principles of the universe. The basic five sounds include the velar 'ㄱ' representing the tongue root blocking the throat, the alveolar 'ㄴ' showing the tongue touching the upper gum, the bilabial 'ㅁ' shaped after the mouth, the dental 'ㅅ' modeled after the teeth, and the glottal 'ㅇ' representing the round throat.

[28] To these five basic letters, I applied the scientific principle of adding strokes as the phonetic intensity increased. A stronger sound than 'ㄴ' became 'ㄷ', and an even harsher sound was systematically created as 'ㅌ' by adding another stroke. This magnificent writing system, which allows one to perceive the strength and lineage of sounds at a glance just by looking at the shapes, is a miracle of featural alphabet unparalleled in human history.

[29] The vowels were modeled after the grand shapes of Heaven, Earth, and Humanity—the three central elements of Eastern cosmology. The fundamental core of these medial sounds consists of the dot 'ㆍ' capturing the round and lofty sky, the horizontal line 'ㅡ' symbolizing the flat and vast earth, and the vertical line 'ㅣ' representing the upright, dignified pillar of humanity.

[30] I created the remaining vowels by combining and synthesizing these three basic vowels according to the harmony of Yin-Yang and the Dual Energies. When the dot combined above or to the right, it became a bright Yang vowel, and below or to the left, a dark Yin vowel, perfectly encompassing the natural order of all sounds. Though the letters are simple, their transformational use and harmony are truly limitless.

[31] Finally, in the autumn of 1446, I completed the compilation of "Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon," featuring the detailed explanations and examples of the eight Jiphyeonjeon scholars led by Jeong In-ji, and officially promulgated it to the world. The grand wisdom of unlocking secrets and accomplishing tasks was achieved on this very day; now, the wise can master it in a single morning, and even the foolish can learn it within ten days to express their thoughts. For the people to finally gain the power of letters and enjoy a just world free of grievances—that was my sole and ultimate desire.

[32] When I fell into deep sorrow after losing my beloved Queen Soheon, I instructed Suyang to compile the biography of Shakyamuni Buddha and translate it into our easy script. That was "Seokbosangjeol," the first translated Buddhist scripture, and upon reading it, I personally composed "Worinchon-gangjigok" to sing its praise. Along with "Yongbieocheonga" celebrating the foundation of Joseon, our new correct sounds carried sacred teachings and quietly spread to the people.

[33] To accelerate the dissemination of the correct sounds, I introduced Hunminjeongeum as a mandatory subject in the examinations for hiring lower-ranking administrative clerks and public officials. I instructed the Ministry of Personnel to select candidates who could at least assemble the letters properly, even if they had not fully mastered Neo-Confucian classics. As clerks at the frontline of administration began using the script, the number of commoners suffering from unjust punishments finally decreased.

[34] Having immersed myself in state affairs my entire life, my physical body was ravaged by diabetes, urinary stones, and boils, and my severe eye disease made it difficult to distinguish a person standing right before me. Although I practically reached blindness, unable to walk even a step in a dark room without a cane, I never laid down my brush and books. While handing over the state affairs to Crown Prince Hyang, I only prayed that if this script could open the eyes and ears of the people, I would gladly offer even the last light of my failing sight.

[35] To draw the final breaths of my life, I relocated to the private residence of my beloved youngest son, Grand Prince Yeong-eung. In the spring of 1450, leaving behind the heavy crown of statehood and lifelong sickness, I finally closed my eyes. To rest forever beside my beloved wife, we were buried together in a single mound at Yeongneung, where I shall eternally pray from heaven that the people enjoy a world free of grievances.

[36] Centuries have passed since I departed this world, and the earnest voice I created for the people has been recognized as a miracle for all humanity. In 1997, UNESCO inscribed the "Hunminjeongeum Haeryebon" on the Memory of the World Register, and annually awards the "King Sejong Literacy Prize" to those who have dedicated themselves to eradicating illiteracy. The pitiful people worldwide who were once trapped in the darkness of illiteracy have finally stepped into the bright light through my letters.

[37] Every year on October 9th, Hangeul Day, the entire nation proudly sings and celebrates the birth of the correct sounds, which deeply moves my heart. Although four characters have vanished into history, the remaining twenty-four letters soar brilliantly as the most rapid and scientific script optimized for the digital information age. Today, as both South and North Korea express their thoughts and communicate through this magnificent script grown from a single root, I continue to live and breathe in every single stroke you write, as an eternal peace of mutual coexistence.
